Venezuelan Freight Surcharge Analysis of Talent Shortage in the Automotive Sector

Venezuela Aviation Authority Imposes Freight Surcharge

The Venezuela Aviation Authority (INAC) implemented a cargo surcharge that went into law on February 3, 2015 and posted in Venezuela's "Official Gazette No. 40,495." Carriers began assessing charges to forwarders on April 14, 2015. As consequence, we must now apply a Venezuela Government Surcharge, effective as of April 27, 2015.

The fees will be assessed as follows:

Destination                              Minimum          Per Kg
CCS                                            $20.00               .20Kg
VLN, BLA, LSP, PMV               $30.00               .30Kg
MAR                                            $20.00               .20Kg

This fee will be applicable to all customers for import freight to Venezuela, regardless of terms, and will be present on all House Airway Bills (HBs), however it does not affect in transit cargo through Venezuela. The accessorial fee code that customers will begin seeing on invoices is GTC. DHL Global Forwarding is committed to keeping our customers informed about changes in government regulations that could potentially impact your business. If you have any questions regarding this surcharge, please reach out to your local sales representative.

Five alternatives to solve the automotive industry talent crisis
DHL recently issued a new white paper on the supply chain talent shortage in the automotive sector – what’s causing it, how it impacts the industry, and what solutions are available to ease the crisis. According to the paper for every graduate with supply chain skills, there are six positions waiting to be filled. The shortage, which will only worsen in the next five years, poses a real threat to the automotive industry’s future, the paper contends. The report is based on research by Lisa Harrington, President of the lharrington group LLC and Senior Research Fellow at the Supply Chain Management Center, Robert H. Smith School of Business, University of Maryland. It provides five alternatives that supply chain executives – and their companies - can employ to address this resource problem: industry collaboration, expanded in-house education options, job rotation programs, formalized knowledge transfer, and employer-of-choice focus.

“The Americas’ automotive industry has seen a lot of progress over the years and is expected to see higher growth in the medium term, but ambitious people are needed to continue this progress,” said Han Roest, former head of Business Development, Automotive for DHL Global Forwarding, Americas. “DHL Global Forwarding serves as one of the auto industry’s primary supply chain partners and, as such, is interested in identifying the knowledge gap and doing its part to find a solution to this talent crisis.”

While many factors contribute to the global supply chain talent gap, the paper highlights five in particular as fueling what some experts are calling the supply chain talent “perfect storm.” In addition to the changing labor market and a growing demographic gap, the academic landscape focused on supply chain management keeps shrinking. Furthermore, particularly students interested in the automotive sector consider a career in supply chain rather as a fallback option. Given this environment, companies operating in the automotive sector should look into adopting new strategies to recruit talent.

To learn more about the crisis and its solutions, download the white paper Solving the talent crisis: Five alternatives every supply chain executive must consider click here.
