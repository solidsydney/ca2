The Spotlight Newsletter

 11/13/2014

U.S. West Coast Port Congestion Update

Since May 2014, the industry has closely monitored the contract negotiations between the International Longshoreman’s and Warehouse Union (ILWU) and the Pacific Maritime Association (PMA). The master labor contract impacts 29 ports, nearly 14,000 union workers and 72 employers on the U.S. West Coast. Collectively, these ports handle more than 50 percent of an estimated twenty million TEU’s moving through U.S. ports on an annual basis.

Throughout the negotiations, the PMA and ILWU have pledged to keep cargo moving through the U.S. West Coast ports despite the absence of a governing contract. The Ports of Tacoma and Seattle also report sustained reductions of 50 percent to 60 percent in terminal productivity due to labor shortages and slowdowns. These circumstances have curtailed yard operations and severely impacted vessel operations at a number of terminals.

The Los Angeles and Long Beach port conditions continue to deteriorate - driven by strong import volume growth of ten percent. The volume combined with a systemic shortage/dislocation of chassis at marine terminals and shortage of skilled labor for container handling equipment has resulted in near gridlock conditions at the ports.

While there are variations by terminal, truckers routinely incur turn times in excess of 4-6 hours. The delays in local cargo continue to average 5 to 10 days or longer depending on the marine terminal and appointment availability. Delays in rail departures due to rail continue to vary widely by carrier and terminal, ranging from 4 to 21 days due to congestion. Cargo vessels are now proceeding to anchor until terminals have sufficient labor and/or terminal capacity to conduct vessel operations.

Outlook:

Demand for vessel capacity to the West Coast Canadian ports, U.S. Gulf and East Coast ports from Asia continue to tighten where many services are now full. DHL’s network of trans-load and warehousing solutions are available to expedite cargo. In addition, DHL’s Air Forward product is available to provide a range of options for customers who have mission critical and time sensitive cargo requiring air freight capacity.

It is DHL’s hope that the ILWU and PMA will continue to negotiate in good faith and swiftly reach a mutually beneficial agreement to provide the basis for long-term stability in the ports and act as a catalyst to resolve the systemic challenges encumbering the U.S. West Coast ports.

DHL expects challenges with service delivery to continue into 2015. Regrettably, pick-up and delivery delays are expected to continue which may result in added cargo costs in the form of storage fees, waiting time, higher delivery costs and dry run fees. DHL is in constant contact with our ocean carriers and trucking partners to develop viable solutions and remains committed to serve our customers during these challenging times. We appreciate your patience and regret any inconvenience these circumstances may cause.

If you have any questions regarding ocean or air contingency routing options, please contact your sales executive or local customer service representative for more information.
